Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Mingyang Sun — msun14@mit.edu

Proposition:

As greenhouse gas emissions continue to rise, the world is experiencing an increase in climate-related disasters. However, the impact is not felt equally—emerging economies, which emit more greenhouse gases, exhibit a stronger correlation between rising emissions and disaster frequency over time, making them more vulnerable to climate risks.

FOR the Proposition

The first visualization demonstrates a strong correlation between total GHG emissions and the frequency of climate-related disasters in emerging economies. As emissions increase, disaster occurrences also rise, particularly after 2012. The stacked bar chart clearly shows that extreme weather events—such as storms, floods, and wildfires—are more frequent in regions with higher emissions. In contrast, advanced economies display a weaker correlation, with a more gradual increase in disasters despite stable or slower emission growth. This suggests that higher emissions in emerging economies contribute to greater climate risks, reinforcing the claim that rising emissions drive disaster frequency.

Decisions and Rationale:

  • Data Aggregation and Transformation: Grouped emissions and disasters by region and year to identify trends. Disasters are stacked by type, ensuring that storm, flood, wildfire, and drought occurrences can be clearly distinguished. (Score: 2)
  • Dual-Axis Chart: Used to emphasize the visual correlation between rising emissions and disasters. And Y axis both start at zero. (Score: 1.5)
  • Color Coding: Different disaster types (droughts, storms, wildfires, floods, etc.) are distinctly colored to highlight their increasing frequency. (Score: 2)

AGAINST the Proposition

The assumption that emerging economies contribute more to climate-related disasters due to higher emissions is challenged by the fact that advanced economies actually emit more greenhouse gases per capita. While emerging economies have higher total emissions due to their larger populations and industrial growth, advanced economies continue to have significantly higher per capita emissions, meaning that each individual in these countries contributes more to global greenhouse gas levels.

The second visualization highlights this contrast by normalizing emissions per million population, revealing that advanced economies have historically emitted more per person while still experiencing climate disasters at a high rate. This suggests that total emissions alone are not the primary driver of disaster frequency, and instead, factors such as urbanization, climate resilience policies, and geographic vulnerabilities play a larger role in determining disaster frequency.

Decisions and Rationale:

  • Per Capita Normalization for Emissions: Instead of focusing on total emissions, this visualization adjusts for population, revealing that advanced economies consistently emit more per person than emerging economies. (Score: 2 - A fair transformation that provides a different yet valid perspective.)
  • Consistent Color Coding for Disaster Types: This design choice reinforces that disaster risks are high in both regions and that advanced economies also experience frequent climate events. (Score: 1)
  • Adjusted Y-Axis Scale: Emissions are plotted on a different scale from disasters, not from zero. (Score: -2)

Data Cleaning and Transformation

The raw data was sourced from the IMF Climate Data Portal and includes two datasets:

  • Annual Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Air Emissions Accounts: Measures annual greenhouse gas emissions (in million metric tons of CO₂ equivalent) from 2010 to 2022, classified by economic groups (Advanced Economies and Emerging and Developing Economies).
  • Climate-related Disasters Frequency: Tracks the frequency of climate-related disasters by country from 1980 to 2024, sourced from the EM-DAT Emergency Events Database. Disaster types include storms, floods, wildfires, droughts, and extreme temperatures.

Data Cleaning and Preprocessing

To ensure consistency and accuracy, the following steps were taken:

  • Filtering Relevant Data: Retained only data from 2010–2022 for emissions and 1980–2024 for disasters. Countries were grouped into Advanced Economies and Emerging and Developing Economies based on IMF classifications.
  • Standardizing Units: Ensured all emissions data was in million metric tons of CO₂ equivalent and disaster occurrences were counted consistently.
  • Data Reshaping: Converted both datasets into long format, where each row represents a combination of Region, Year, Emissions, Disaster Type, and Disaster Frequency.

Data Transformation and Aggregation

After cleaning, data transformation was performed to prepare for visualization:

  • Summing Emissions by Region and Year: Aggregated emissions for each economic group to analyze trends over time.
  • Grouping Disaster Occurrences: Disaster frequency was summed for each region and year to compare trends with emissions.
  • Normalizing Disaster Frequency: An additional transformation was performed to calculate disaster frequency per million population, ensuring a fair comparison between regions with different population sizes.
  • Merging the Two Datasets: Joined emissions and disaster frequency data on Region and Year, enabling a direct comparison of emissions and climate disasters.

Final Reflection

Going through the design process for both visualizations, I realized just how much data transformations and visual choices shape people’s interpretations. Cleaning and organizing the data—grouping emissions and disasters by region and year—was relatively straightforward, making it easier to compare trends. However, representing the relationship between emissions and disasters proved more challenging. There was a fine line between making the visuals persuasive and maintaining ethical visualization practices. One of the toughest decisions was whether to use absolute emissions or normalize them per capita. The first approach highlighted a strong correlation between emissions and disaster frequency, while the second shifted the focus to per capita emissions, challenging that assumption. This contrast reinforced how valid but different transformations can lead to vastly different interpretations.

This project reshaped my understanding of ethical data visualization. It’s not just about accuracy but also about ensuring data is presented transparently, without misleading the audience through selective framing or deceptive scaling. There’s a crucial distinction between enhancing clarity and manipulating data to push a specific narrative. For instance, using a dual-axis plot to highlight relationships can be effective, but exaggerating trends by tweaking scales crosses an ethical boundary. Similarly, filtering data can improve readability, but omitting key patterns to fit a particular message is misleading. Ultimately, ethical visualization should provide a fair, truthful representation of data, empowering viewers to draw their own informed conclusions rather than subtly steering them toward a biased perspective.
