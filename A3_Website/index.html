Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • New cases’ Peak in Early 2022: The widest section of the red shaded area appears in January 2022, indicating a record-breaking surge in COVID-19 cases.
  • Peaks appear around winter (January–February) in both 2021 and 2022, aligning with seasonal surges.
  • General increasing patterns over years: each successive year shows an increase in peak case numbers, with 2022 being the highest, followed by 2021, and 2020 having the smallest peaks.

Design Critique:

  • The spiral timeline is a visually striking but unconventional way to present COVID-19 case trends. The red shaded area effectively highlights the 7-day average, showing fluctuations in daily case numbers. However, the format makes it difficult to compare exact case counts across different years, as there is no numerical scale to provide precise values.
  • Another drawback is the difficulty in tracking seasonal trends and daily fluctuations. The spiral layout forces readers to follow time in a circular pattern, which may not be intuitive, especially for those unfamiliar with this format.
  • Additionally, the design gives the impression that case numbers exploded in early 2022, but it is hard to directly compare this with the peaks from January to February 2021. If the spiral continues expanding, future peaks might either overlap or create too much empty space, making it tricky to balance clarity and spacing. The choice of the spiral’s radius is therefore crucial in maintaining readability while ensuring an accurate representation of trends.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

In redesigning the COVID-19 case visualization, I aimed to retain the engaging radial format while improving interpretability and clarity. The primary issue with the original spiral design was its difficulty in comparing data across years, as the case counts were not explicitly labeled and seasonal trends were hard to discern. To address these concerns, I chose a circular stacked barplot as the final design, which maintains the visual appeal of the radial format but enhances readability. The stacked bars allow for direct year-over-year comparison, making it easier to see how case numbers evolved over time. I also ensured that the spacing and scaling of the bars were carefully adjusted to avoid excessive overlap while still maintaining a compact layout. The circular layout preserves the seasonal storytelling aspect of the data while providing a clearer view of how case trends shift annually.

In terms of visual encodings, I used color to differentiate years, ensuring that each period is distinguishable while maintaining visual continuity. The bar lengths represent case counts, scaled appropriately to emphasize peaks and seasonal trends without overwhelming the viewer with excessive detail. A logarithmic transformation was considered but ultimately not used, as the focus was on absolute case magnitude rather than proportional change. Compared to the original spiral, the circular stacked barplot allows for better quantitative comparisons while preserving the engaging structure.

Reflection

The final visualization aims to effectively communicate the seasonal patterns and year-over-year differences in COVID-19 cases in the U.S. from 2020 to 2022. By using a circular stacked barplot, I sought to emphasize the recurring winter peaks while providing a more structured comparison of different years. The stacked format enables direct year-over-year analysis, making it easier to identify trends like the increasing peak case counts over time. This design maintains the engaging radial structure of the original but enhances readability, ensuring that both the seasonal fluctuations and long-term growth of cases remain evident. The choice of a categorical color scheme for different years aids in distinguishing trends while ensuring that each year’s data remains visually coherent.

Reflecting on the redesign process, I found that the critique phase helped uncover key trade-offs in visualization design. While my final design improves comparability and numerical clarity, it does sacrifice some of the fluidity of the spiral format, which effectively conveyed the cyclical nature of outbreaks. Additionally, while my visualization provides a clearer structure, fine-grained day-to-day variations are less prominent than in a line-based graph. This iterative process reinforced the balance between aesthetics and data precision—an ongoing challenge in designing effective visualizations.
